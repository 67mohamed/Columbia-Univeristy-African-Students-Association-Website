ASA Executive Board

Rowa Eltohami

President

Economics, MESAAS '17

As a member of this community, I’ve learned, unlearned, and relearned. I’ve been inspired, re-educated, and strengthened. I’ve grown so much as a leader, from all the experiences, conversations and people I’ve come across. I’ve developed a sense of debt to the people who initially gave the ASA community a voice and presence on Columbia's campus.

Akosua Bekoe

Vice President

Sustainable Development '18

Akwaaba! (Welcome in Twi) I am Ghanaian yet American. American yet Ghanaian. The two societies in which I have been raised present stark contradictions in ideology, contradictions which I have deciphered and adjusted to create my own unique dogma. I am the product of the vibrant colors of the Kente cloth and the stripes of the American flag.

Frederick Kofi Tam

Secretary

Computer Engineering '18

I’m from Ghana/Btswana and lived in Africa all my life before coming to Columbia and as such, my African identity has strongly influenced the person I am today and the person I am becoming. I love my culture and I believe without it, I would be lost and confused.. I joined ASA to keep me in touch with my roots and to find a community I could feel at home in.

John Anukem

Treasurer

Computer Science '18'

I’m from both Baltimore Maryland, and Arlington Texas, but raised by two well versed Nigerians. I’m a firm believer that understanding where you come from is key in becoming who you want to be. More than Jollof and Alade, I love learning about the culture that has shaped my experiences thus far. If you don’t see me on top of a chair at KDR, then you’ll probably find me behind three monitors in NoCo.

Zuwaina Ateig

Mareting Chair

Sophomore in Columbia College

I’m from Wadi Halfa, Sudan, and I was raised in Abu Dhabi. Growing up in a predominantly Arab community, I wondered why the Arabic dialect I spoke was different, why my hair was bigger than everyone else’s, why the shade of my skin was seen as a shortcoming. Africa has never made me feel that way, and neither has ASA. ASA has been - much like Africa - a home that doesn’t exclude.

Omnia Saed

Campus Liaison

Political Science, MESAAS '18

I am from Northern Virginia by way of Sudan. Having lived in the States for most of my life, understanding my identity, who I am, and where I come from are questions that have plagued by experience as an African Immigrant, black, Muslim woman. ASA has answered many of these questions; ASA has allowed me to understand the malleability of identity and the beauty of the diaspora.

Oluwayemisi Olorunwunmi

Legacy Chair

African Studies, Political Science '18

My name is Oluwayemisi Olorunwunmi; however, I give you permission to call me Yemi. I joined Columbia’s African Students Association because being a child of the motherland will always be one of the premier parts of my identity. I always think back to where I’m from, the values of my community, the self-determination of my people because it lets me know where I am going.

Bradley

Freshman Representative

Hey! My name is Bradley Mensah, ASA’s First-Year Rep! I’m American by birth and Togolese by blood. It wasn’t until my family and I moved there four years ago that I was able to experience what it means to be Togolese, and on a grander scale, what it means to be African. Being a part of ASA has helped me continue to shape my identity as a Togolese man and has exposed me to the beautiful diversity on the continent.

Ruguruw Nerima

Freshman Representative

My name is Karira, Natalie Ruguru. I was born and raised in Kenya. I had never had to question my identity until I was taken out of the place where I grew up with people who looked like me. ASA for me is where I get to learn about other parts of the continent from my peers and let them in on my secret that is my home.

Simone Ige

Political Chair

Comparative Literature & Society, MESAAS '18

I am British-Ugandan but grew up in Uganda and Mozambique. I joined ASA to find a community where I could feel at home in order to be able to survive and thrive at this University. ASA has certainly been that as well as being a space that has constantly pushed me to critically engage with the facets of my identity as a diasporic African woman in America.

Chimsom Orakwue

Marketing Chair

Psychology, PreMed '17

I am a Nigerian American, a simple statement that, yet, holds so much depth and meaning- for one to be truly appreciative and proud of their own culture. My identity within the African American and American African was something I often grappled with; I was part of both and yet part of neither at the same time. However, the past years I have learned that one does not necessarily have to exist without the other.

I am Nigerian American that loves everything about my culture from the food to the people. I joined ASA to learn more about Nigerian and further my education about other areas in Africa. ASA has become a large part of my experience at Columbia and I would not change it for the world.
